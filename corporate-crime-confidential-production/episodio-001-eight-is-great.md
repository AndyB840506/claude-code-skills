# CASE FILE No. 001: Eight Is Great
Corporate Crime Confidential — Pilot Episode
Duration target: ~45 min | ~6,800 words | Format: solo, word-for-word

**Sourcing note (read before recording):** Every fact, figure, date, and quoted line in this
script is drawn from court/regulatory filings, sworn testimony, or verified reporting — see
the source list at the end. The two first-person sections are dramatized narration: they
voice documented decisions and public statements as internal monologue. Where a real name is
used (Carrie Tolstedt, John Stumpf), the actions and quoted lines are real and cited; the
interior voice around them is a reconstruction, not a transcript. The frontline banker
character is a composite built from multiple documented, reported cases — not a named
individual, since no single identity is confirmed in the sourcing.

[SFX: distant hum of a call center floor, phones ringing, fades under voice]

Eight products.

That's all they asked for. Eight products, every customer, every single day. Checking.
Savings. A credit card. Online bill pay. A line of credit. Doesn't matter if they need it.
Doesn't matter if they asked for it. Eight.

[PAUSA]

You want to know how you get there — really get there, every day, without missing quota —
when the honest number is two, maybe three? I'll tell you exactly how. I did it for four
years before it caught up with me.

[PAUSA]

This is the story of how a bank turned its own employees into the fraud — and then fired
them for it.

── SEGMENT 3: CONTEXT ──────────────────────────────────── (~4 min · ~600 words)

Nineteen ninety-nine. Wells Fargo sets a number. Eight. Eight financial products, in every
single household they bank. Checking account, savings account, credit card, debit card,
home loan, investment account, and so on. The internal name for it was "cross-sell." The
slogan they put on posters in break rooms was "Going for gr-eight." [ÉNFASIS] Eight is
great. You were supposed to say it like it was fun.

[PAUSA]

The man who owned that number was the CEO, John Stumpf. Wall Street loved him for it. Wall
Street called him "the King of Cross-Sell" — that's a real nickname, from a real report, not
something I'm dressing up for you. [VERIFICAR: exact original coining, cited to a 2016
watchdog report titled "The King of Cross-Sell"]. And on paper, the logic wasn't insane.
A customer with eight products at one bank is a customer who never leaves. Loyalty,
dressed up as a number.

[PAUSA]

Except a number, once you make it the only thing that gets you promoted — or fired — stops
measuring anything. It becomes the thing itself. And when the person running the division
that owned that number was someone who'd spend the next two decades being praised for
hitting it... well. That's a different character in this file. We'll get to her.

[PAUSA]

First, you need to meet the person actually holding the number every single day. Not in a
boardroom. On a phone, at a branch, at nine in the morning, with a manager standing behind
them checking a whiteboard.

[TRANSICIÓN]

── SEGMENT 4: PART A — THE BANKER ──────────────────── (~12 min · ~1,800 words)

[NOTA: composite voice — first person, present tense where possible, conversational,
rationalizing]

My name doesn't matter. There were fifty-three hundred of us, by the time they were done
counting. [ÉNFASIS] Fifty-three hundred. That's how many of us got fired over about five
years for "sales practice violations." That's the term they used. Not fraud. Violations.
Like a parking ticket.

[PAUSA]

Here's how it actually worked, on the ground, at a branch that looked exactly like the one
near you.

Every morning, before the doors opened, there was a whiteboard. Your name, and a number next
to it. Not your sales for the month — your sales for [ÉNFASIS] today. If you didn't hit it
by close, your manager pulled you aside. Not to yell. Worse — to ask you, quietly, what was
going on. Was there a problem at home. Were you not a good fit here. The threat was never
spoken. It didn't need to be.

[PAUSA]

So you learn. Everyone learns. Here's lesson one: "pinning." A customer opens a checking
account — fine, that's real, that's one product. Now you need it to count as two. So you
set up a debit card PIN for them right there at the desk, without asking, because "setting
up the PIN" counts as activating a second product. They never asked for a PIN. They don't
even know you did it. But it's on the sheet.

Lesson two: you need email enrollment to count as a product too. Customer doesn't have an
email, or doesn't want to give you one? You make one up. "NoName" at Wells Fargo dot com.
[VERIFICAR: exact address format reported, but the practice itself — inventing fake
customer emails to enroll accounts in online banking — is confirmed in reporting on the
scandal.] It's not their email. It'll never reach them. It doesn't matter. It counts.

Lesson three, and this is the one that got people fired and prosecuted: you just open the
account. Full stop. Savings account, nobody asked for it, no signature — or a signature you
have from another form, moved onto this one. A credit card, same thing, mailed to an
address that's already on file so it doesn't look strange when it arrives. The customer
finds out three ways: a fee hits their real account and they call confused, or a credit
inquiry dings their score and they call angry, or they never find out at all.

[PAUSA]

I want to be honest about something, because this file is supposed to be honest. Nobody
handed me a memo that said "commit fraud." Nobody in a suit ever said the word "fake" to my
face. What happened was smaller than that, and that's what made it work: every single
individual step felt like a shortcut, not a crime. Pinning felt like paperwork. The email
thing felt like a technicality. It was only when you added up thirty of those a month, times
a few thousand of us, times years, that it turned into two million accounts nobody asked
for. [ÉNFASIS] Nobody adds it up when they're the one doing it. That's the whole trick of
this kind of fraud — it's invisible from inside a single transaction, and it's a crime wave
from the outside.

[PAUSA]

There was a phone number. An ethics hotline. Call this number if you see something wrong,
they told us, in training, on posters, in the handbook. I know someone who called it. Not
me — I'll get to me. He worked in Pennsylvania. He refused to open a fake account one
afternoon, went home, and that night, called the hotline and reported what he'd been asked
to do.

He was fired a few days later. [VERIFICAR: exact number of days, per reporting — but the
core fact, a banker fired shortly after an ethics-hotline call reporting the practice, is
documented.] Officially, for something else. There's always something else — a late clock-
in, a paperwork error, anything in a personnel file that can be pointed to instead of the
real reason. Federal regulators later found this happened again and again: people who blew
the whistle got managed out, quietly, while the number on the whiteboard kept climbing.

[PAUSA]

Me — I didn't call anybody for three and a half years. I hit the number. I told myself the
customer wasn't really harmed, the account would just sit there, unused, and get closed
eventually, no fees, no foul. That's what you tell yourself. That's the whole script running
in your head, every single day, for years. Then one day a woman came in furious, waving a
statement, asking why she had a savings account she never opened, with a monthly fee she'd
been eating for eight months because she never checked that statement.

[PAUSA]

I remember looking at her account screen and doing the math in my head — how many of those
had I opened that year alone — before I even answered her question.

[SFX: brief silence, one beat]

That's segment one of this file. A whiteboard, a fake email address, and fifty-three
hundred people who took the fall first.

[TRANSICIÓN]

── SEGMENT 5: THE DATA BRIDGE ──────────────────────── (~4 min · ~600 words)

Here's what the record actually shows, in numbers, no dramatization needed.

September 2016: regulators — the Consumer Financial Protection Bureau, the Office of the
Comptroller of the Currency, and the Los Angeles City Attorney — announce a combined one
hundred and eighty-five million dollar fine against Wells Fargo. The bank confirms it has
opened more than two million deposit and credit card accounts that customers never
authorized. And it confirms something else: over the preceding five years, it had already
fired fifty-three hundred employees for exactly the sales practices we just heard described.

[ÉNFASIS] Fifty-three hundred people, quietly let go, years before the public ever heard
the number two million.

[PAUSA]

Four months later, January 2017: Wells Fargo eliminates individual sales quotas entirely.
Which is, itself, an admission. If removing the quota fixes the problem, the quota was the
problem.

[PAUSA]

CEO John Stumpf's first public instinct, in a Wall Street Journal interview, was to point at
the fifty-three hundred: [ÉNFASIS] "If they're not going to do the thing that we ask them to
do — put customers first, honor our vision and values — I don't want them here." [VERIFICAR:
quote as reported by multiple outlets covering the 2016 scandal response]

He said that about the people who were following a whiteboard.

[PAUSA]

Weeks later, in prepared testimony before the Senate Banking Committee, he reversed course
completely: [ÉNFASIS] "I accept full responsibility for all unethical sales practices in our
retail banking business." Senator Pat Toomey, in that same hearing, said what a lot of
people were already thinking — that he found it hard to believe fifty-three hundred
employees had all decided, independently, with no direction from above, to commit the exact
same kind of fraud in branches across the entire country.

[PAUSA]

If fifty-three hundred people didn't invent this on their own — someone built the system
they were operating inside of. That's the next file.

[TRANSICIÓN]

── SEGMENT 6: PART B — TOLSTEDT ──────────────────── (~12 min · ~1,800 words)

[NOTA: named individual — Carrie Tolstedt, former head of Wells Fargo's Community Bank
division. Every action described below is charged/documented conduct per SEC and OCC
filings. Internal reflection is dramatized narration built around that documented conduct.]

My name is Carrie Tolstedt. For most of two decades, I ran the Community Bank at Wells
Fargo — the branches, the tellers, the personal bankers, the whole retail machine you just
heard from. I didn't invent the number eight. I inherited it. What I did was defend it, in
public, to investors, for years, after I already knew what it was costing to hit.

[PAUSA]

Here's what the regulators say I did, and I'm not going to soften it, because softening it
is exactly what I was charged with doing the first time.

From twenty fourteen through twenty sixteen, I stood in front of investor conferences and
described our cross-sell metric — the average number of products per household — as a
mark of the bank's health. I signed certifications, attached to public filings, vouching
for the accuracy of what we were telling investors and regulators. [ÉNFASIS] I did that
while aware of internal reports of misconduct inside the Community Bank. That's not my
interpretation. That's the SEC's language, in the complaint I settled.

[PAUSA]

You want to know what it feels like from inside, when the number is going up and the
complaints are also going up, at the same time? It feels like two different realities that
you get to choose between, every morning, and I chose the one where the number was real.
I told myself the sales practice issues were an execution problem — bad apples, poor
training, a handful of branches — not a structural one. I told myself that because the
alternative was admitting the entire cross-sell strategy, the thing I'd built my career
on, the thing Wall Street loved me for, was rotten at the root. [ÉNFASIS] Nobody wants to
be the one who says that about the machine they run.

[PAUSA]

There's a difference between what happened on the phone with a customer at nine in the
morning, and what happened in a boardroom at nine in the morning, and it's this: the banker
on the floor was afraid of losing a job that paid maybe forty thousand dollars a year. I
was defending a metric that justified my compensation, my promotions, my standing with a
CEO who called himself the King of Cross-Sell because of numbers my division produced.
[ÉNFASIS] The incentive at the top of the pyramid and the incentive at the bottom of it
were pointed in exactly the same direction — hit the number, don't ask what it costs — they
just had very different price tags attached.

[PAUSA]

When the fifty-three hundred firings started becoming public, my instinct — and I want to
be precise about this instead of dressing it up — was containment, not correction. Keep the
story at the branch level. Keep it about individual employees making individual bad choices.
Because the moment it becomes a story about the metric itself, it becomes a story about the
people who owned the metric. And I owned it longer than anyone.

[PAUSA]

In twenty twenty, federal regulators filed charges against me directly — not the bank,
me — for misleading investors about what that cross-sell number actually meant. I didn't
admit guilt in that settlement. But by twenty twenty-three, the picture had changed enough
that I pleaded guilty to a different charge: obstructing a bank regulator's examination.
Not "I didn't know." [ÉNFASIS] Obstructing the people whose job was to find out.

[PAUSA]

I paid the SEC three million dollars, plus disgorgement of the money I'd made, plus
interest. The OCC fined me seventeen million and barred me from banking for life. A federal
judge sentenced me to three years of probation.

[PAUSA]

No prison. Not one day.

[SFX: one beat of silence]

Compare that, if you want, to the fifty-three hundred people who lost their jobs first,
years before anyone knew my name.

[TRANSICIÓN]

── SEGMENT 7: THE VERDICT GAP ──────────────────────── (~4 min · ~600 words)

Let's put both outcomes next to each other, plainly, because this is the part of the file
that should actually bother you.

The frontline employees: fired, mostly without severance, mostly without the resources to
fight it, some of them fired specifically [ÉNFASIS] for reporting the fraud through the
channel the company told them to use. Two of them later filed a class action seeking two
point six billion dollars in damages for what happened to their careers and reputations.
[VERIFICAR: current status/outcome of that specific litigation at time of recording — check
before publishing.] A separate whistleblower, fired in twenty ten for reporting fraud
through that same hotline, had to go to a federal agency — OSHA — just to get five point
four million dollars in back pay and damages, years after the fact.

[PAUSA]

Carrie Tolstedt: three million to the SEC, seventeen million to the OCC, a lifetime ban from
banking, three years of probation. [ÉNFASIS] No prison.

[PAUSA]

I'm not telling you this to say the fine wasn't real, or that probation is nothing. Twenty
million dollars is real money, and a lifetime ban is a real professional death sentence for
someone who spent a career climbing to run a division of the largest bank in the country.

But sit with the shape of it. The people closest to the harm — the ones who actually opened
accounts customers didn't ask for, however wrong that was — paid with their jobs, sometimes
their reputations, in some cases while they were the ones trying to stop it. The person who
ran the system that made that the rational, career-preserving choice for fifty-three hundred
different people, independently, across the entire country — paid with money and a
supervised sentence.

[PAUSA]

That's not a coincidence of two unrelated cases. That's what it actually looks like when a
company's incentive structure works exactly as designed, gets caught, and then the
consequences get distributed the way consequences always get distributed in organizations —
downward, first, and hardest.

[TRANSICIÓN]

── SEGMENT 8: THE TAKEAWAY ──────────────────────── (~4 min · ~600 words)

Here's what I want you to actually take out of this file, past the outrage, past the
numbers.

Every operation runs on incentives someone designed on purpose. A whiteboard with a number
on it is not neutral. It is a leadership decision, dressed up as a metric, and it will
produce exactly the behavior it rewards — not the behavior a mission statement claims to
want. Wells Fargo's own values poster, hanging in every branch that ran this scheme, talked
about putting customers first. The whiteboard next to it said something completely
different, and the whiteboard won, every single day, because the whiteboard is what
determined who kept their job.

[PAUSA]

If you lead people — a team, a floor, a department, it doesn't matter the size — the
question this file leaves you with isn't "would I ever do what Tolstedt did." Almost nobody
believes they would. The real question is smaller, and harder to see in the moment: what
are you measuring right now that people under you might be quietly gaming, in ways too
small for you to notice individually, because the alternative is a bad review or a lost
job? Pinning felt small. A fake email felt small. That's exactly how a bad incentive hides —
never as one obvious crime, always as a thousand tiny, reasonable-feeling shortcuts.

[PAUSA]

And if you're the one under the number — the lesson isn't "never cut a corner," because
you already know that, and it didn't stop fifty-three hundred people who knew it too. The
lesson is that the system protects the people who design incentives far better than it
protects the people who live inside them. That's not an excuse for what happened on that
banking floor. It's a warning about where to actually put your attention before you're the
one explaining yourself to a regulator, or a reporter, or your own conscience.

[PAUSA]

What we do — in a boardroom, or at a teller window — echoes. For good, or for bad. The
only real choice anyone in this file had was which direction they wanted their echo to run.
Most of them didn't get to choose consciously. That's the whole tragedy of this case. By the
time you notice the whiteboard is the thing actually running your life, you've already been
answering to it for years.

[TRANSICIÓN]
