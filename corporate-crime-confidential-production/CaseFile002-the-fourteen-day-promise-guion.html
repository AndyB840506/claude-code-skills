Segment 3 · Context~4.5 min

Here's the number this whole file turns on: fourteen.

The Department of Veterans Affairs promised that a veteran asking for a new appointment would be seen within fourteen days. Not as a hope. As a performance measure — the kind that shows up in executives' performance plans, the kind that feeds ratings, salary increases, bonuses. The people running VA hospitals were graded, in part, on hitting fourteen.

Pausa

And fourteen wasn't even the original promise. For years the standard had been thirty days — and in 2011, the department tightened it to fourteen, because its own data showed the thirty-day goal being met over ninety-five percent of the time. Data that, as we're about to see, was already fiction. People inside warned the new target wasn't attainable with the staffing they actually had; the White House's own review would later call it flatly "unrealistic." Think about that sequence: the goal was being "met" on paper, so leadership made the goal harder — and kept everyone's pay attached to it. They tightened a promise based on numbers the promise itself had corrupted.

Now hold that number against reality. Phoenix, Arizona — one of the fastest-growing veteran populations in the country. Vietnam-era patients aging into heavy care. New patients coming home from Iraq and Afghanistan. And a primary-care operation that did not have anywhere near the doctors to see every new patient in two weeks. The demand and the capacity didn't match. Everyone inside could see it.

Pausa

So the metric had a choice to make, and metrics always make the same choice: it stopped measuring reality and started replacing it.

Here's what makes this file different from an ordinary scandal, though. The VA knew. In writing. On April 26, 2010 — four years before any of this became news — a VA deputy under secretary named William Schoenhard sent a memo to the entire system. His words: "It has come to my attention that in order to improve scores on assorted access measures, certain facilities have adopted use of inappropriate scheduling practices." Attached to the memo: eight pages describing twenty-four distinct tricks — his office called them "gaming strategies" — that facilities were using to make wait times look shorter than they were. Twenty-four. Cataloged. Named. With a warning that they would not be tolerated.

Pausa

Think about what that memo actually is. It's the institution writing down, in detail, every way its own people were faking the number — and then keeping the number, keeping the incentives attached to the number, and filing the memo. If you're trying to stop a fraud, that's a warning. If you're not — it reads like a manual.

Pausa

Four years later, at Phoenix, it was the manual.

If you heard Case File No. 001 — the bank, the whiteboard, the eight products — you already know the disease we're looking at tonight. Same pathogen, different organ. A target nobody can hit honestly, tied to pay, checked by nobody. The only real difference is what was sitting at the bottom of the funnel. At the bank it was fake accounts.

Here it was people.

Let me introduce you to the person holding the fourteen every single day. Not a director. Not a doctor. A scheduling clerk, at a desk, with a phone that never stopped ringing.

Segment 4 · Part A — The Scheduler~15 min
Composite voice — first person, conversational, rationalizing. Built from the scheduling practices documented in the OIG reports and whistleblower accounts.

My name doesn't matter. I was a scheduling clerk at the Phoenix VA. GS-5, GS-6 — the bottom of the federal ladder. My job was simple: veteran calls, veteran needs a doctor, I put the veteran in the system, the system gives them a date. That's the job I was hired for.

That is not the job I was trained for.

Pausa

My first week, someone walked me through what they called — and I want you to hear the phrase, because the phrase is doing a lot of work — "how we do it here." Nobody said falsify. Nobody said hide. In four years, I never once heard a word that would sound bad in a deposition. What I heard was: this is how we manage access. This is how we keep the numbers where they need to be. This is how we do it here.

Here's how we did it here.

Pausa

A veteran calls. New patient. Needs primary care. The honest move is to put him straight into the electronic waiting list — the official one, the one Washington can see — with today's date. The clock starts today. And if the first open slot is in July, the system records the truth: this man waited seven months.

We did not do the honest move.

Instead, you take his information and you set it aside. Maybe you print the screen and it goes in a folder. Maybe it goes on a paper list, or a spreadsheet, or a shared drive — every office had its own flavor. What matters is one thing: he does not go into the official system today. Officially, as far as the Department of Veterans Affairs knows, this man does not exist yet.

Pausa

Then you wait. Weeks. Months. You watch the schedule, and when a slot finally opens up inside the next fourteen days — that's when you enter him. You create him in the system on Monday, you book him for the following Thursday, and the computer does the math it was built to do: this veteran waited ten days.

Ten days. For a man who sat in my folder since March.

Pausa

The clock doesn't start when the veteran asks for help. The clock starts when I say he asked. That's the entire scheme. That's it. No hacked computers, no shredded ledgers, no offshore anything. Just a delay in when a name gets typed in. It doesn't even feel like lying while you're doing it — it feels like timing.

And there was a second trick, for the veterans we did enter right away. The system asked for the patient's "desired date" — when does he want to be seen. You'd have him on the phone, he'd say "as soon as possible," and you would look at the calendar, find the first open slot — say, June the tenth — and you'd ask him, gently: "Does June the tenth work for you?" He says yes. And you type June the tenth into the box marked desired date.

Do you see it? On paper, this veteran got exactly what he wanted, on exactly the day he wanted it. Wait time: zero. The seven months between his phone call and that slot never happened, statistically. He wanted June the tenth. We gave him June the tenth. The Schoenhard memo had a name for that one — it was in the twenty-four. It was still running, four years after the memo said it wouldn't be tolerated.

Pausa

And those were just our house specialties. That eight-page catalog had tricks we never even needed. Somewhere in the system, facilities were canceling appointments on fine print — show up less than ten or fifteen minutes early, lose your slot — and every cancellation, conveniently, could restart a clock. Twenty-four ways to make a waiting room disappear into a database, and the memo itself admitted the list probably wasn't complete. When the tricks outnumber the fingers you have to count them on, you're not looking at bad apples. You're looking at a craft. A trade skill, passed desk to desk, with its own vocabulary and its own apprenticeship — mine started my first week, remember.

And here's the thing about a craft: it survives inspection, because inspection is part of the craft. When word came down that someone from the region was coming through, the office got careful. Not honest — careful. You learn the difference fast. The folder got thinner for a week. Anyone who might have said something out loud already knew better; you didn't need to threaten people, you just needed everyone to understand that the numbers were the facility's numbers, and the facility signed your performance review. The people who eventually did talk — you'll notice, when we get there — talked after they retired. That's not a coincidence. That's the design.

Pausa

I want to walk you through why I did it, because "she was evil" is a lazy answer and this show doesn't do lazy answers.

First: I was trained to do it, by people who were trained to do it, in an office where it was as normal as the coffee machine. When every colleague you have does a thing, and your supervisor teaches the thing, and the facility's numbers depend on the thing, it stops looking like a decision. It looks like the job.

Second: the number was watched. Not casually — structurally. The fourteen-day figure rolled up from my keyboard into the clinic's numbers, from the clinic into the facility's scorecard, from the facility to the region, from the region to Washington. Somewhere up that chain, people I never met had performance plans, ratings, and bonus money tied to what I typed. Nobody up there called to ask if the veterans were okay. The phone rang when the number slipped. And when the number slipped, the pressure came back down the same chain it went up, and it landed on the last person holding it — which was me.

Third — and this is the one I actually told myself at the time — I never denied anyone care. That was my line. I wasn't canceling appointments. I wasn't turning anyone away. Every man and woman in that folder was going to get seen, eventually, the same day they would have gotten seen anyway — the capacity didn't change because of where I wrote their name. All I moved was paperwork. All I shifted was when a database found out. That's what I told myself, and I want to be honest with you: for about three and a half years, it worked. I slept fine.

Pausa

Here's what that line ignores, and I knew it ignored it.

The waiting list wasn't just a queue. It was the visibility. A veteran on the official list is a data point someone in Washington can count, question, staff against. Seventeen hundred veterans in folders and spreadsheets are invisible. Nobody allocates doctors for a folder. Nobody flags the chest-pain patient in a drawer. The inspector general would later use a phrase for those seventeen hundred that I think about more than I'd like to: "at risk of being lost or forgotten." Not a metaphor. An operational description. We had built a system whose entire function was to make waiting veterans statistically invisible — and then we were surprised that some of them slipped through it.

Pausa

And the calls came in. That's the part that doesn't fit in a spreadsheet. The same veteran, calling back, month after month: "Still nothing?" You learn phrases. "You're in our system." True — my system, not the system. "We'll call you as soon as something opens up." Also true. And the big one, the release valve, the sentence that let all of us sleep: "If you feel this is an emergency, hang up and go to the emergency room." Which sounds like care, until you understand what it actually is — it's the queue outsourcing its conscience. Anything truly urgent will self-select out of my folder and into the ER, so whatever stays in the folder must, by definition, be fine. Every sentence technically true. I got very good at sentences that were technically true.

Pausa

CNN later told the country about a seventy-one-year-old Navy veteran — Thomas Breen. End of September 2013, he was rushed into our emergency room with blood in his urine and a history of cancer. Seen, sent home, with his chart marked "urgent" — see a doctor about this within one week. He died on November 30th. Stage four bladder cancer. On December 6th, the VA called his family — they finally had an appointment for him. His family got to say it out loud to the machine: "Really — you're a little too late." I'm not going to claim him for my folder — I don't know whose queue he sat in. I'm telling you about him because "urgent, within one week" was a sentence our system could read, print, and file — and still convert into a phone call to a dead man. That's what a gamed queue does that a slow queue doesn't: a slow queue at least knows who it's failing.

Some of the men on my list were in their eighties. Korea. Vietnam. Some had caregivers calling for them, daughters, wives, and the wives were always politer than they had any right to be. You'd hang up and the folder was right there in the drawer, and you'd think: he's number two hundred and something. And then the phone rang again and you stopped thinking it.

Pausa

The day it cracked for me was small. They're always small. I was updating the list — housekeeping, we maintained our shadow list like professionals, that's the detail that should tell you everything — and there was a name I couldn't reach. Phone disconnected. And a colleague, not even looking up, said maybe check the obituaries. Like it was a known step. Like it had happened before.

It had happened before.

Pausa

I don't know if the men on my particular list who died would have lived with faster care. Neither do you. Neither, it turns out, does the inspector general of the United States — and we'll get to exactly what his report could and couldn't say, because that sentence is the darkest thing in this file.

What I know is this: the number fourteen went up the chain from my desk, every week, clean and green. And it was fiction. I wrote fiction, for four years, with a government keyboard — and my performance reviews were excellent.

Segment 5 · The Data Bridge~5.5 min

Here's what the record actually shows, in dates and numbers, no dramatization needed.

Fall 2013: a doctor named Sam Foote — twenty-four years at the Phoenix VA, newly retired, which matters, because the people still inside were afraid — writes to the VA's Office of Inspector General. He describes the scheme: an official list showing timely care, and veterans parked off the books, waiting months, some dying before they were ever seen. The response does not end the practice. So in March 2014, he writes again.

Pausa

Then he stops writing letters. On April 23, 2014, Dr. Foote goes on CNN and says it on camera: as many as forty veterans may have died waiting for care at Phoenix, hidden on a secret list. And what two letters to an inspector general couldn't do, one television interview does in weeks.

May 16, 2014: Dr. Robert Petzel, the VA's top health official, retires early — at the request of Secretary Eric Shinseki.

May 28, 2014: the inspector general publishes an interim report, and it is worse than the allegation. About seventeen hundred veterans at Phoenix, waiting for primary care, are on no official waiting list at all — in the OIG's own words, "at risk of being lost or forgotten." The 226 veterans the OIG sampled had waited an average of 115 days for their first appointment — 84 percent of them past the fourteen-day promise — while Phoenix's senior officials were reporting an average wait of 24 days. And the report says the word that turns a local story into a national one: the manipulation is "systemic." Within days, 42 VA medical centers are under investigation. Phoenix wasn't the crime. Phoenix was the sample.

Pausa

May 30, 2014: Secretary Eric Shinseki — a retired four-star general, a wounded combat veteran himself — resigns.

June 2014: the VA's acting secretary, Sloan Gibson, tells reporters that of those seventeen hundred unlisted Phoenix veterans, eighteen died before anyone ever called them back.

That same month, the VA audits itself nationwide, and Phoenix stops being the story. More than 57,000 veterans across the country are found waiting over ninety days for a first appointment — and about 64,000 more, enrolled over the previous decade, had simply never been seen at all. And buried in that same audit, the sentence that vindicates everything you heard in Part A: schedulers at ninety separate clinics admitted altering desired dates — and, in virtually all cases, said they'd been instructed to, by supervisors. This was not one hospital's drawer. It was the drawer, everywhere, taught as procedure.

August 7, 2014: the President signs the Veterans Access, Choice, and Accountability Act — more than fifteen billion dollars to buy the capacity the metric had been faking.

Pausa

And then, August 26, 2014: the final OIG report. This is the sentence I told you about. The inspector general confirms the scheme — the secret lists, the gamed dates, all of it. He identifies veterans who experienced "clinically significant delays" in care, and six of them died. But on the question everyone wanted answered — did the delays kill them — the report says this: "we are unable to conclusively assert that the absence of timely quality care caused the deaths of these veterans."

Pausa

Read that sentence the way an operations person reads it. It is not an acquittal. It's a description of what the fraud destroyed. When you erase the real waiting list, you erase the evidence chain. You can't prove the wait killed a man if the system was built so the wait officially never happened. The scheme didn't just hide the queue — it hid the body count, permanently, in both directions. Nobody can prove it. Nobody can disprove it.

The perfect crime isn't the one nobody can solve. It's the one nobody can measure.

Pausa

So: seventeen hundred invisible veterans, a systemic finding, a secretary gone, fifteen billion dollars. Somebody ran this hospital while all of that built up.

Segment 8 · The Takeaway~5.5 min

Here's what I want you to take out of this file, past the anger, into whatever you actually run — a team, a clinic, a call center, a company.

First. When a target is impossible with the capacity you've funded, people will not miss the target. They will fix the measurement. Every time. Phoenix could not see new patients in fourteen days — that was a staffing fact, a budget fact, a geometry fact. The fourteen-day promise didn't change the geometry. It just moved the lie to the cheapest place to hide it: a data-entry field. If you remember one sentence from tonight: an impossible target doesn't produce heroic effort. It produces fiction with your logo on it.

Pausa

Second. A warning memo is not a control. The VA didn't fail to notice the gaming — it cataloged the gaming, twenty-four strategies deep, four years early, and then changed nothing about the incentive that caused it. If you name the disease and keep the diet, you haven't issued a warning. You've published documentation. The Schoenhard memo should haunt every leader who's ever fired off a "reminder about our values" email and considered a risk handled. Ask yourself what your organization has already warned itself about, in writing, and left armed.

Pausa

Third — and this is the operational core — the control that would have caught this was almost embarrassingly simple: reconcile demand against the queue. Phone calls came in every day. Referrals came in every day. That intake existed — it just never got compared against the official waiting list. Seventeen hundred people is not a rounding error; it's a gap you'd catch in an afternoon with two spreadsheets, if anyone independent ever put the two spreadsheets side by side. Nobody did, because the only people with both spreadsheets were the people the metric was grading. Never let the graded party be the sole custodian of the measurement. That one sentence, applied, unravels this entire file — and Case File No. 001, and half the files we will ever open on this show.

If you want the Monday-morning version: pick the one metric your organization brags about most, and trace it backwards — not from the dashboard down, but from the raw intake up. The customer's first email. The patient's first call. The timestamp nobody's bonus depends on. If the story that raw intake tells matches the dashboard, sleep well. If there's a gap — and you'll know within an afternoon — you've found your folder in a drawer. Every organization has one. The only question is whether you find it, or a reporter does.

Pausa

And fourth, the uncomfortable one. Watch your best numbers, not your worst. The instinct is to audit the failing clinic, the red dashboard, the missed quota. But fraud doesn't live in the red. Fraud lives in the green — in the unit that never misses, quarter after quarter, no matter what happens to staffing or demand. Phoenix looked fine. Phoenix looked better than fine; its director was getting performance bonuses. If a number stays perfect while the world around it gets harder, that's not excellence. That's usually authorship.

Pausa

The men in that folder wore the uniform of this country. They did the waiting; the number never waited a day. And the system that promised them fourteen days spent years measuring, rewarding, and defending everything about that promise except whether it was true.

What you measure is a promise. Fund it, verify it independently, or don't make it — because someone downstream is holding your metric with a phone that won't stop ringing, and you've already taught them what happens if the number slips.
