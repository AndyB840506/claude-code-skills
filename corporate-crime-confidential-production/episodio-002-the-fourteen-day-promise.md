# CASE FILE No. 002: The Fourteen-Day Promise
Corporate Crime Confidential — Episode 2
Duration target: 40–45 min | Format: solo, word-for-word

**Sourcing note (read before recording):** Every fact, figure, date, and quoted line in this
script is drawn from the VA Office of Inspector General's interim and final Phoenix reports,
Merit Systems Protection Board rulings, court records, congressional testimony, and verified
reporting — see the source list at the end. The two first-person sections are dramatized

Where a real name is used (Sharon Helman, Dr. Sam Foote, William Schoenhard), the actions and

transcript. The scheduler character in Part A is a composite built from the documented

guardrail is load-bearing for this episode: Sharon Helman was never convicted of falsifying
wait lists, and this script never puts that admission in her mouth. Her documented conduct —
and the striking absence of any conviction for the fraud itself — is the story.

── SEGMENT 3: CONTEXT ──────────────────────────────────── (~4 min · ~500 words)

Here's the number this whole file turns on: fourteen.

The Department of Veterans Affairs promised that a veteran asking for a new appointment
would be seen within fourteen days. Not as a hope. As a performance measure — the kind
that shows up in executives' performance plans, the kind that feeds ratings, salary
increases, bonuses. The people running VA hospitals were graded, in part, on hitting
fourteen.

[PAUSA]

And fourteen wasn't even the original promise. For years the standard had been thirty
days; the department tightened it to fourteen even as people inside warned the target
wasn't attainable with the staffing they actually had. [VERIFICAR: the 30-to-14
tightening and internal warnings, per NBC's "Performance Mismanagement" reporting —
confirm the exact year before recording.] Think about that sequence: the goal was
already being missed, so leadership made the goal [ÉNFASIS] harder — and attached more
of everyone's pay to it.

Now hold that number against reality. Phoenix, Arizona — one of the fastest-growing
veteran populations in the country. Vietnam-era patients aging into heavy care. New
patients coming home from Iraq and Afghanistan. And a primary-care operation that did not
have anywhere near the doctors to see every new patient in two weeks. The demand and the
capacity didn't match. Everyone inside could see it.

[PAUSA]

So the metric had a choice to make, and metrics always make the same choice: it stopped
measuring reality and started replacing it.

Here's what makes this file different from an ordinary scandal, though. The VA [ÉNFASIS]
knew. In writing. On April 26, 2010 — four years before any of this became news — a VA
deputy under secretary named William Schoenhard sent a memo to the entire system. His
words: "It has come to my attention that in order to improve scores on assorted access
measures, certain facilities have adopted use of inappropriate scheduling practices."
Attached to the memo: eight pages describing [ÉNFASIS] twenty-four distinct tricks —
his office called them "gaming strategies" — that facilities were using to make wait
times look shorter than they were. Twenty-four. Cataloged. Named. With a warning that
they would not be tolerated.

[PAUSA]

Think about what that memo actually is. It's the institution writing down, in detail,
every way its own people were faking the number — and then keeping the number, keeping
the incentives attached to the number, and filing the memo. If you're trying to stop a
fraud, that's a warning. If you're not — it reads like a manual.

[PAUSA]

Four years later, at Phoenix, it was the manual.

If you heard Case File No. 001 — the bank, the whiteboard, the eight products — you
already know the disease we're looking at tonight. Same pathogen, different organ. A
target nobody can hit honestly, tied to pay, checked by nobody. The only real difference
is what was sitting at the bottom of the funnel. At the bank it was fake accounts.

[ÉNFASIS] Here it was people.

Let me introduce you to the person holding the fourteen every single day. Not a director.
Not a doctor. A scheduling clerk, at a desk, with a phone that never stopped ringing.

── SEGMENT 4: PART A — THE SCHEDULER ──────────────────── (~12.5 min · ~1,700 words)

[NOTA: composite voice — first person, conversational, rationalizing. Built from the
scheduling practices documented in the OIG reports and whistleblower accounts.]

My name doesn't matter. I was a scheduling clerk at the Phoenix VA. GS-5, GS-6 — the
bottom of the federal ladder. My job was simple: veteran calls, veteran needs a doctor,
I put the veteran in the system, the system gives them a date. That's the job I was
hired for.

That is not the job I was trained for.

[PAUSA]

My first week, someone walked me through what they called — and I want you to hear the
phrase, because the phrase is doing a lot of work — [ÉNFASIS] "how we do it here."
Nobody said falsify. Nobody said hide. In four years, I never once heard a word that
would sound bad in a deposition. What I heard was: this is how we manage access. This
is how we keep the numbers where they need to be. This is how we do it here.

Here's how we did it here.

[PAUSA]

A veteran calls. New patient. Needs primary care. The honest move is to put him straight
into the electronic waiting list — the official one, the one Washington can see — with
today's date. The clock starts today. And if the first open slot is in July, the system
records the truth: this man waited seven months.

We did not do the honest move.

Instead, you take his information and you set it aside. Maybe you print the screen and
it goes in a folder. Maybe it goes on a paper list, or a spreadsheet, or a shared drive
— every office had its own flavor. What matters is one thing: [ÉNFASIS] he does not go
into the official system today. Officially, as far as the Department of Veterans Affairs
knows, this man does not exist yet.

[PAUSA]

Then you wait. Weeks. Months. You watch the schedule, and when a slot finally opens up
inside the next fourteen days — that's when you enter him. You create him in the system
on Monday, you book him for the following Thursday, and the computer does the math it
was built to do: this veteran waited ten days.

Ten days. For a man who sat in my folder since March.

[PAUSA]

The clock doesn't start when the veteran asks for help. The clock starts when I say he
asked. That's the entire scheme. That's it. No hacked computers, no shredded ledgers, no
offshore anything. Just a delay in when a name gets typed in. It doesn't even feel like
lying while you're doing it — it feels like [ÉNFASIS] timing.

And there was a second trick, for the veterans we did enter right away. The system asked
for the patient's "desired date" — when does he want to be seen. You'd have him on the
phone, he'd say "as soon as possible," and you would look at the calendar, find the first
open slot — say, June the tenth — and you'd ask him, gently: "Does June the tenth work
for you?" He says yes. And you type June the tenth into the box marked [ÉNFASIS] desired
date.

Do you see it? On paper, this veteran got exactly what he wanted, on exactly the day he
wanted it. Wait time: zero. The seven months between his phone call and that slot never
happened, statistically. He wanted June the tenth. We gave him June the tenth. The
Schoenhard memo had a name for that one — it was in the twenty-four. It was still
running, four years after the memo said it wouldn't be tolerated.

[PAUSA]

And those were just our house specialties. That eight-page catalog had tricks we never
even needed. Somewhere in the system, facilities were canceling appointments on fine
print — show up less than ten or fifteen minutes early, lose your slot — and every
cancellation, conveniently, could restart a clock. Twenty-four ways to make a waiting
room disappear into a database, and the memo itself admitted the list probably wasn't
complete. When the tricks outnumber the fingers you have to count them on, you're not
looking at bad apples. You're looking at a [ÉNFASIS] craft. A trade skill, passed
desk to desk, with its own vocabulary and its own apprenticeship — mine started my
first week, remember.

And here's the thing about a craft: it survives inspection, because inspection is part
of the craft. When word came down that someone from the region was coming through, the
office got careful. Not honest — [ÉNFASIS] careful. You learn the difference fast.
The folder got thinner for a week. Anyone who might have said something out loud
already knew better; you didn't need to threaten people, you just needed everyone to
understand that the numbers were the facility's numbers, and the facility signed your
performance review. The people who eventually did talk — you'll notice, when we get
there — talked [ÉNFASIS] after they retired. That's not a coincidence. That's the
design.

[PAUSA]

I want to walk you through why I did it, because "she was evil" is a lazy answer and
this show doesn't do lazy answers.

First: I was trained to do it, by people who were trained to do it, in an office where
it was as normal as the coffee machine. When every colleague you have does a thing, and
your supervisor teaches the thing, and the facility's numbers depend on the thing, it
stops looking like a decision. It looks like the job.

Second: the number was watched. Not casually — [ÉNFASIS] structurally. The fourteen-day
figure rolled up from my keyboard into the clinic's numbers, from the clinic into the
facility's scorecard, from the facility to the region, from the region to Washington.
Somewhere up that chain, people I never met had performance plans, ratings, and bonus
money tied to what I typed. Nobody up there called to ask if the veterans were okay.
The phone rang when the number slipped. And when the number slipped, the pressure came
back down the same chain it went up, and it landed on the last person holding it —
which was me.

Third — and this is the one I actually told myself at the time — [ÉNFASIS] I never
denied anyone care. That was my line. I wasn't canceling appointments. I wasn't turning
anyone away. Every man and woman in that folder was going to get seen, eventually, the
same day they would have gotten seen anyway — the capacity didn't change because of
where I wrote their name. All I moved was paperwork. All I shifted was when a database
found out. That's what I told myself, and I want to be honest with you: for about three
and a half years, it worked. I slept fine.

[PAUSA]

Here's what that line ignores, and I knew it ignored it.

The waiting list wasn't just a queue. It was the [ÉNFASIS] visibility. A veteran on the
official list is a data point someone in Washington can count, question, staff against.
Seventeen hundred veterans in folders and spreadsheets are invisible. Nobody allocates
doctors for a folder. Nobody flags the chest-pain patient in a drawer. The inspector
general would later use a phrase for those seventeen hundred that I think about more
than I'd like to: [ÉNFASIS] "at risk of being lost or forgotten." Not a metaphor. An
operational description. We had built a system whose entire function was to make
waiting veterans statistically invisible — and then we were surprised that some of
them slipped through it.

[PAUSA]

And the calls came in. That's the part that doesn't fit in a spreadsheet. The same
veteran, calling back, month after month: "Still nothing?" You learn phrases. "You're
in our system." True — my system, not the system. "We'll call you as soon as something
opens up." Also true. And the big one, the release valve, the sentence that let all of
us sleep: "If you feel this is an emergency, hang up and go to the emergency room."
Which sounds like care, until you understand what it actually is — it's the queue
outsourcing its conscience. Anything truly urgent will self-select out of my folder and
into the ER, so whatever stays in the folder must, by definition, be fine. Every
sentence technically true. I got very good at sentences that were technically true.

[PAUSA]

CNN later told the country about a Navy veteran in his seventies — Thomas Breen. He
came into our emergency room with blood in his urine and a history of cancer. Seen,
stabilized, sent home with a note in his chart: follow-up needed, urgent. The
follow-up call came [ÉNFASIS] after he was dead. Bladder cancer. His family got to
hear the VA calling to schedule an appointment for a man they had already buried.
[VERIFICAR: Breen case details as reported by CNN's original April 2014 investigation
— confirm name, age, and sequence against the CNN piece before recording.] I'm not
going to claim him for my folder — I don't know whose queue he sat in. I'm telling
you about him because "urgent" was a word our system could read, print, and file —
and still convert into a phone call to a dead man. That's what a gamed queue does
that a slow queue doesn't: a slow queue at least knows [ÉNFASIS] who it's failing.

Some of the men on my list were in their eighties. Korea. Vietnam. Some had caregivers
calling for them, daughters, wives, and the wives were always politer than they had any
right to be. You'd hang up and the folder was right there in the drawer, and you'd
think: he's number two hundred and something. And then the phone rang again and you
stopped thinking it.

[PAUSA]

The day it cracked for me was small. They're always small. I was updating the list —
housekeeping, we maintained our shadow list like professionals, that's the detail that
should tell you everything — and there was a name I couldn't reach. Phone disconnected.
And a colleague, not even looking up, said maybe check the obituaries. Like it was a
known step. Like it had happened before.

[ÉNFASIS] It had happened before.

[PAUSA]

I don't know if the men on my particular list who died would have lived with faster
care. Neither do you. Neither, it turns out, does the inspector general of the United
States — and we'll get to exactly what his report could and couldn't say, because that
sentence is the darkest thing in this file.

What I know is this: the number fourteen went up the chain from my desk, every week,
clean and green. And it was fiction. I wrote fiction, for four years, with a government
keyboard — and my performance reviews were [ÉNFASIS] excellent.

[SFX: brief silence, one beat]

That's the floor of this file. A folder, a desired-date box, and seventeen hundred
people who officially weren't waiting.

Now let's climb.

── SEGMENT 5: THE DATA BRIDGE ──────────────────────── (~4.5 min · ~600 words)

Here's what the record actually shows, in dates and numbers, no dramatization needed.

which matters, because the people still inside were afraid — writes to the VA's Office of

response does not end the practice. So in March 2014, he writes [ÉNFASIS] again.

[PAUSA]

Then he stops writing letters. On April 23, 2014, Dr. Foote goes on CNN and says it on
camera: as many as forty veterans may have died waiting for care at Phoenix, hidden on a
secret list. And what two letters to an inspector general couldn't do, one television
interview does in weeks.

May 16, 2014: Dr. Robert Petzel, the VA's top health official, retires early — at the
request of Secretary Eric Shinseki.

May 28, 2014: the inspector general publishes an interim report, and it is worse than
the allegation. About [ÉNFASIS] seventeen hundred veterans at Phoenix, waiting for
primary care, are on no official waiting list at all — in the OIG's own words, "at risk
of being lost or forgotten." The veterans the OIG sampled had waited an average of 115
days for their first appointment, while the facility reported waits of about 24 days.
[VERIFICAR: the 115-day sample average is in the interim report; confirm the reported
~24-day official figure against the report text before recording.] And the report says
the word that turns a local story into a national one: the manipulation is [ÉNFASIS]
"systemic." Within days, 42 VA medical centers are under investigation. Phoenix wasn't
the crime. Phoenix was the sample.

[PAUSA]

May 30, 2014: Secretary Eric Shinseki — a retired four-star general, a wounded combat
veteran himself — resigns.

June 2014: the VA's own deputy secretary tells Congress that of those seventeen hundred
unlisted Phoenix veterans, [ÉNFASIS] eighteen died before anyone ever called them back.
[VERIFICAR: figure attributed to Deputy Secretary Sloan Gibson in June 2014 reporting —
confirm attribution before recording.]

That same month, the VA audits itself nationwide, and Phoenix stops being the story.
Across the country, tens of thousands of veterans are found waiting more than ninety
days for a first appointment — and tens of thousands more, enrolled over the previous
decade, had simply [ÉNFASIS] never been seen at all. [VERIFICAR: the June 2014

approximately 64,000 enrolled-but-never-seen — confirm both figures against the audit

It was the drawer, everywhere.

August 7, 2014: Congress passes the Veterans Access, Choice, and Accountability Act —
roughly sixteen billion dollars to buy the capacity the metric had been faking.
[VERIFICAR: commonly reported as $16.3 billion — confirm figure.]

[PAUSA]

And then, August 26, 2014: the final OIG report. This is the sentence I told you about.
The inspector general confirms the scheme — the secret lists, the gamed dates, all of

six of them died. But on the question everyone wanted answered — did the delays kill

timely quality care caused the deaths of these veterans."

[PAUSA]

Read that sentence the way an operations person reads it. It is not an acquittal. It's
a description of what the fraud [ÉNFASIS] destroyed. When you erase the real waiting
list, you erase the evidence chain. You can't prove the wait killed a man if the
system was built so the wait officially never happened. The scheme didn't just hide
the queue — it hid the body count, permanently, in both directions. Nobody can prove
it. Nobody can disprove it.

The perfect crime isn't the one nobody can solve. It's the one nobody can [ÉNFASIS]
measure.

[PAUSA]

So: seventeen hundred invisible veterans, a systemic finding, a secretary gone, sixteen
billion dollars. Somebody ran this hospital while all of that built up.

Let's meet her.
